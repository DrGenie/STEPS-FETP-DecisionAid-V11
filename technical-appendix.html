Part A – For decision-makers and FETP leaders

1. What problem does STEPS help you solve?

India has trained around 3,300 field epidemiologists across the Epidemic Intelligence Service (EIS) and FETP streams. The International Health Regulations (IHR, 2005) and WHO’s Joint External Evaluation use an indicative target of one trained field epidemiologist per 200,000 people for workforce development.1 For a population of about 1.3 billion, this implies a long-run need for roughly 6,500 Intermediate or Advanced-level field epidemiologists in India.

The “One India” FETP roadmap, launched by NCDC in 2023, sets out a vision to bring all FETP streams under one umbrella and extend field epidemiology coverage to all districts by 2030, using a three-tier cascade from Frontline to Intermediate and Advanced programmes.2

❓
Key question for decision-makers
Given budget limits, mentor capacity, and the IHR target, which mix of FETP tiers and scales delivers the biggest health security gains per rupee spent, without overloading mentors or institutions?

STEPS helps answer that question by combining:

  • preferences from a discrete-choice experiment (DCE) on what stakeholders value in FETP design;
  • simple, cost and epidemiological benefit calculations; and
  • a clear view of mentor and site capacity constraints.

2. How should I move through the STEPS tabs?

  • Step 1 – Start with the story and the options.
    Use the Introduction and Attributes and levels tabs to understand what each tier of FETP is for, and what can be adjusted: scale (cohorts and trainees), mentorship intensity, delivery mode, qualification type, and costs.
  • Step 2 – Set the rules of the game.
    On the Settings tab, set the planning horizon and discount rate, the value placed on outbreak responses, and the number of mentors and sites available. These assumptions shape all results.
  • Step 3 – Configure one scenario.
    In the Configuration tab, choose a tier, mentorship intensity, delivery mode, qualification type, cohort size, number of cohorts, and monthly cost per trainee. Click Apply configuration to generate a full scenario.
  • Step 4 – Read results and feasibility.
    The Results, Costing details and National simulation tabs summarise endorsement, perceived programme value, economic costs, epidemiological benefits, benefit–cost ratios (BCRs) and mentor feasibility.
  • Step 5 – Stress-test endorsement and value.
    The Perceived programme value / Sensitivity tab answer: "Do our preferred options remain robust if we are more conservative about benefits?”
  • Step 6 – Save, compare and export.
    The Saved scenarios tab keeps your own scenarios and a separate set of Top-5 recommended scenarios generated by the model. The Copilot briefing tab then turns these into ready-to-use text and prompts for policy notes, presentations and reports.

3. How does each STEPS tab work in practice?

3.1 Introduction – “What does this tool actually do for me?”

The Introduction tab answers three basic questions:

  • Why invest now? It links FETP scale-up to IHR (2005), JEE findings, and the One India FETP roadmap.
  • What can we change? It explains the levers: scale, mentorship, delivery mode, qualification, and costs.
  • What does “good” look like? It clarifies that STEPS is not a forecasting tool but a structured way to compare options transparently.

Colour cues used throughout the tool are introduced here: Green for broadly favourable, feasible options; Amber where trade-offs need discussion; and Red where cost or capacity is a serious concern.

3.2 Attributes and levels – “What features does the model recognise?”

This tab summarises the key attributes used in the DCE and in the scenario engine:

  • Programme tier: Frontline (≈3 months), Intermediate (≈12 months), Advanced (≈24 months).
  • Mentorship intensity: Low, medium, high – affecting quality and how many trainees each mentor can support.
  • Delivery mode: Fully in-person or blended – affecting costs and perceived programme value.
  • Qualification: Certificate vs university qualification – influencing career incentives and recognition.

For each attribute, the tab briefly notes how stakeholders responded in the DCE – for example, that higher mentorship and a recognised university qualification typically increased endorsement but at higher cost.

3.3 Settings – “Under what assumptions do these numbers hold?”

The Settings tab defines the ground rules:

  • Planning horizon and discount rate. The default is 1 year and 3%, but you can extend the horizon for multi-year roadmaps. All net present value (NPV) calculations update automatically when you change these inputs.
  • Epidemiological value assumptions. Defaults specify the completion rate, the expected number of outbreak responses per graduate per year, and the indicative monetary value per averted outbreak (for example, completion 90%, 0.5 outbreak responses per graduate per year, and a nominal value of INR 4,000,000 per significant response). These can be updated when new evidence is available.
  • Capacity and costs. Here you enter the number of mentors and training sites realistically available, and any base mentor support cost per cohort. These inputs drive the feasibility status and mentor shortfall metrics across the tool.
ℹ
When you change a setting…
Every time you apply a new assumption, STEPS logs the change and recomputes value, cost, NPV and feasibility for all active and saved scenarios, as well as for the Top-5 recommended scenarios.

3.4 Configuration – “How do I build a scenario?”

The Configuration tab is where you build one policy package at a time by:

  • choosing a tier (Frontline, Intermediate, Advanced);
  • selecting mentorship intensity, delivery mode and qualification type;
  • setting trainees per cohort and the number of cohorts; and
  • entering monthly cost per trainee and any mentor support cost per cohort.

STEPS applies simple coherence checks so that tier duration, planning horizon and cohorts match logically:

  • Frontline: ≈3 months. With a 1-year horizon, up to 4 cohorts are feasible (default capped at 4).
  • Intermediate: ≈12 months. With a 1-year horizon, one full cohort per site at a time is realistic.
  • Advanced: ≈24 months. The tool enforces a minimum 2-year horizon and appropriate cohort limits.

After you click Apply configuration, the tool generates:

  • a qualitative message such as Promising configuration, Needs discussion or Challenging configuration, based on endorsement, BCR and whether willingness-to-pay (WTP) proxies exceed costs; and
  • mentor and site capacity metrics, including required mentors per cohort, required mentors nationally and mentor shortfall;
  • updated costs, benefits and NPVs used in the Results, Costing, National simulation, and Saved scenarios tabs.

3.5 Results – “How strong is support and is it worth the money?”

The Results tab answers two questions:

  • How much support is there? Endorsement is the predicted share of stakeholders who would support the configuration, based on DCE preference estimates and the selected attribute levels.
  • Is it good value for money? Perceived programme value converts preferences into a rupee-equivalent WTP measure, which is compared with economic costs to produce net benefit and BCR.

The tab also shows a short feasibility summary, indicating whether the option is within current mentor and site capacity, the size of any mentor shortfall, and a brief narrative explaining whether low endorsement, high cost, or capacity constraints are the main concern.

3.6 Costing details – “Where does the money go?”

The Costing details tab unpacks total economic costs per cohort and nationally into intuitive components such as:

  • trainee-related costs (e.g. stipends, travel, accommodation where included);
  • mentor support costs, based on per-cohort inputs and the number of mentors implied by mentorship intensity; and
  • institutional or university charges where applicable.

These components sum exactly to the economic cost lines shown in the Results and National simulation tabs, ensuring consistency.

3.7 National simulation – “What if we scale this nationally?”

The National simulation tab asks: “If we take this configuration and implement it across India, what happens?” It:

  • scales costs and benefits to the selected number of cohorts and planning horizon;
  • reports required mentors per cohort, required mentors nationally, available mentors nationally, and mentor shortfalls; and
  • summarises feasibility as Within capacity or Requires capacity expansion, with a short explanation.

3.8 Perceived programme value / Sensitivity – “Do our preferred options remain robust if we are more conservative about benefits?”

This tab is designed for structured “what-if?” questions. It:

  • shows baseline endorsement and perceived programme value for the current configuration; and
  • then shows how different definition of benefits change the results.

Epidemiological benefits are intentionally not shown here so that decision-makers can focus on stakeholder support and perceived value.

3.9 Copilot briefing – “How do I turn results into a narrative?”

The Copilot briefing tab automatically builds:

  • a concise summary of the current configuration;
  • a structured description of all user-saved scenarios; and
  • a separate summary of the Top-5 recommended scenarios generated by the model logic (value for money and feasibility), clearly labelled as Recommended scenarios.

It then combines these into prompts for Copilot and ChatGPT to generate policy briefs, slide decks or more detailed reports. Buttons copy the prompts to the clipboard, open Copilot or ChatGPT in a new tab, and provide a downloadable text or Word file. If there are no saved scenarios, the pop-up warning appears in red to prevent empty or misleading outputs.

3.10 Saved scenarios – “How do I compare options and export?”

The Saved scenarios tab is the control room. It has:

  • Your saved scenarios: Each saved configuration appears in a table with endorsement, perceived programme value, economic cost, epidemiological benefits (where used), net benefit, BCR, feasibility status and mentor shortfall. Automatically generated Enablers and Risks are stored and shown for each scenario and used in exports.
  • Top-5 recommended scenarios: Independently computed from the model logic and clearly labelled as Recommended scenarios. They never overwrite user-saved scenarios; they sit alongside them.

Export options include:

  • Standard export: multi-page PDF or Word summary of all saved scenarios, enablers, risks and capacity implications;
  • Brief export (two pages): concise PDF or Word note centred on the Top-5 recommendations and any shortlisted user scenarios, with Enablers and Risks boxes auto-filled; and
  • Top-5 export: PDF, Word or Excel outputs containing only the five model-recommended scenarios for rapid discussion.

5. Moving from 3,300 to 6,500 field epidemiologists

The IHR target of one field epidemiologist per 200,000 population implies:

Required epidemiologists ≈ 1,300,000,000 / 200,000 = 6,500

If the current stock is around 3,300 Intermediate/Advanced equivalent epidemiologists, the additional requirement is roughly 3,200 graduates over the next several years.

5.1 Illustrative pathway – focusing on Intermediate and Advanced

As an illustration (not an official plan), suppose that between now and 2030 India runs, across a national network:

  • 10 Intermediate cohorts per year, each with 35 trainees;
  • 6 Advanced cohorts per year, each with 25 trainees; and
  • a completion rate of 90% for both tiers.

The annual flow of Intermediate/Advanced graduates would be:

Graduates per year = 0.9 × (10 × 35 + 6 × 25) = 0.9 × (350 + 150) = 0.9 × 500 = 450

Sustained over seven years:

450 × 7 = 3,150 additional graduates

Adding these to the existing ≈3,300 would bring India very close to the 6,500 benchmark. STEPS allows you to refine these numbers under different budget envelopes and mentor capacities.

5.2 What does this imply for mentors?

Using the mentorship assumptions above:

  • Intermediate: N_cohort = ceil(35 / 3.5) = 10 mentors per cohort.
  • Advanced: N_cohort = ceil(25 / 2) = 13 mentors per cohort.

With 10 Intermediate and 6 Advanced cohorts per year:

Mentors required per year (slots) = 10 × 10 + 13 × 6 = 100 + 78 = 178

This is a count of “mentor slots”, not unique individuals. Some mentors can support more than one cohort over time. In STEPS you can enter your own estimates of available mentors and immediately see whether a proposed pathway is within capacity or requires targeted mentor development.

5.3 Additional portfolio examples

Beyond the three single-tier options, STEPS is designed to compare multi-tier portfolios. For example:

  • Portfolio A – Frontline-heavy expansion: multiple Frontline cohorts across states to boost basic surveillance quickly, with a smaller Intermediate stream.
  • Portfolio B – Balanced cascade: moderate Frontline expansion, strong Intermediate backbone, modest Advanced stream.
  • Portfolio C – Leadership-focused: prioritised Intermediate and Advanced cohorts to build a leadership cadre first, before large-scale Frontline expansion.

In practice, users construct these portfolios by saving several scenarios in STEPS (Frontline, Intermediate, Advanced) and then using the Saved scenarios and Top-5 panels to compare value for money, feasibility and mentor requirements side-by-side.

Part B – Technical structure and computations

6. Core configuration variables

For each scenario, STEPS stores the following key variables:

  • Tier: g ∈ {frontline, intermediate, advanced}.
  • Programme duration in months: M_g (3, 12, 24 respectively).
  • Mentorship intensity: m ∈ {low, medium, high}.
  • Trainees per cohort: T.
  • Number of cohorts: C.
  • Monthly cost per trainee: c_trainee.
  • Mentor support cost per cohort: c_mentor (may be zero).
  • Available mentors nationally: A_mentor.
  • Planning horizon in years: H.
  • Discount rate (annual): r (default 0.03).

7. Mentor capacity and shortfall

Mentorship intensity is mapped to an implied number of trainees per mentor using a simple rule:

f(m) = 2 if m = high; 3.5 if m = medium; 5 if m = low

Required mentors per cohort are:

N_cohort = ceil( T / f(m) )

The total mentor requirement (ignoring detailed scheduling) is:

N_total = N_cohort × C

Given available mentors A_mentor, the mentor shortfall is:

Shortfall = max( 0, N_total − A_mentor )

Worked example – Option 1 (Frontline)

With medium mentorship (f(m) = 3.5), T = 35 and C = 4:

N_cohort = ceil(35 / 3.5) = ceil(10) = 10

N_total = 10 × 4 = 40

If A_mentor = 200, then Shortfall = max(0, 40 − 200) = 0, so the configuration is within mentor capacity.

8. Economic costs

Ignoring additional sub-components, the core cost definitions are:

Direct trainee cost per cohort:

Cost_trainee, cohort = T × M_g × c_trainee

Mentor support cost per cohort:

Cost_mentor, cohort = c_mentor

Total cost per cohort:

Cost_cohort = Cost_trainee, cohort + Cost_mentor, cohort + overheads

where “overheads” captures any institutional or university-level charges that are specified in the input templates.

Total cost across cohorts:

Cost_total = C × Cost_cohort

Worked example – Option 2 (Intermediate)

With T = 35, M_g = 12, c_trainee = 100,000 and C = 3:

Cost_trainee, cohort = 35 × 12 × 100,000 = 42,000,000 INR

Cost_total (trainee component only) = 3 × 42,000,000 = 126,000,000 INR

Mentor support costs and overheads are then added to obtain total economic cost.

9. Epidemiological benefits and NPV

For each tier g, epidemiological parameters include:

  • Completion rate: pi_g (e.g. 0.9).
  • Outbreak responses per graduate per year: lambda_g (e.g. 0.5).
  • Value per outbreak response: v_out,g (e.g. 4,000,000 INR).
  • Additional value per graduate (if used): v_grad,g (default 0).

Graduates per cohort:

G_cohort = pi_g × T

Total graduates:

G_total = G_cohort × C

Outbreak responses per year (one cohort):

Outbreaks_per_year, cohort = G_cohort × lambda_g

Annual outbreak benefit per cohort:

B_out, annual = Outbreaks_per_year, cohort × v_out,g

Present value factor over H years at rate r:

PV_factor = sum over t = 1 to H of [ 1 / (1 + r)^t ]

PV of outbreak benefits per cohort:

B_out, PV, cohort = B_out, annual × PV_factor

PV of graduate benefits per cohort (if used):

B_grad, PV, cohort = G_cohort × v_grad,g

Total epidemiological benefit per cohort (with cross-sector multiplier k):

B_epi, cohort = k × ( B_out, PV, cohort + B_grad, PV, cohort )

Multiplying by C gives the total epidemiological benefit for the scenario.

Worked example – Option 3 (Advanced)

For illustration: T = 25, pi_advanced = 0.9, lambda_advanced = 0.5, v_out, advanced = 4,000,000, H = 5, r = 0.03.

G_cohort = 0.9 × 25 = 22.5

Outbreaks_per_year, cohort = 22.5 × 0.5 = 11.25

B_out, annual = 11.25 × 4,000,000 = 45,000,000 INR

PV_factor (5 years, 3%) ≈ 4.58

B_out, PV, cohort ≈ 45,000,000 × 4.58 ≈ 206,100,000 INR

10. Perceived programme value and endorsement (DCE)

The DCE estimates preference weights beta_k for attribute levels x_k. For a given configuration, a utility index is:

U = beta_0 + Σ_k ( beta_k × x_k )

Endorsement is modelled as a logistic transformation of the difference between this utility and a reference option with utility U_ref:

Endorsement = 1 / ( 1 + exp( − ( U − U_ref ) ) )

Perceived programme value is obtained by dividing all beta coefficients by a cost coefficient beta_cost to convert utilities into rupee-equivalent WTP. This WTP is then scaled by cohort size and number of cohorts to produce an indicative monetary value that can be compared with economic costs.

11. Net benefit and benefit–cost ratio

STEPS reports net benefit and BCR in two ways:

  • using perceived programme value (WTP-based); and
  • using epidemiological benefits (outbreak and graduate values).

For any definition of benefit B and cost C:

Net benefit = B − C

BCR = B / C

These metrics are calculated at cohort level and at “national” level given the number of cohorts and the planning horizon.
